                                       PREFACE
1. Purpose
This multi-Service tactics, techniques, and procedures (MTTP) publication for dynamic
targeting (DT) is a single source, descriptive reference guide to assist component and
Service commanders and their staffs in using DT to plan, target, and process respective
targets in all domains and environments. This MTTP is used by Services and
components in joint and multinational combined operations, including operating with
other United States (US) departments and agencies.
2. Scope
This MTTP publication highlights tactics, techniques, and procedures (TTP) common to
DT, presents best practices, and includes key lessons learned from combat and major
exercises. It describes commanders’ objectives and guidance; multi-Service command
and control, execution procedures; and planning, coordinating, organizing, and training
for DT. It also outlines multinational considerations, collaborative tools, and DT checklist
samples.
3. Applicability
This MTTP publication applies to all commanders and their staffs that participate in DT.
The target audience includes commanders and staffs, Service component current
operations, fires, future plans, and intelligence sections, and joint/combined component
forces. This MTTP applies to the JFC’s staff, the respective operations centers, the
Service liaison officers (LNO), fire support personnel, and operators at all echelons.
This publication is intended to be used as a reference document for developing
interoperability with other US departments and agencies, allies, and coalition partners
when integrating with US military forces.

                                       Chapter I
                                     OVERVIEW
1. Introduction
   a. The Joint Targeting Cycle (JTC). The JTC is a sequence of targeting tasks that
   incorporates the entire procedure from target nomination through engagement and
   assessment. It provides an essential framework to conduct the joint targeting
   process. It is composed of six interrelated phases which are neither rigidly
   sequential nor constrained by time. The JTC supports deliberate and dynamic
   targeting (DT) and provides the necessary flexibility when the operational
   environment (OE) forces a change in commander’s intent and concept of operations
   (CONOPS). These are the six phases in the JTC:
      (1) Phase 1. Commander’s objectives, targeting guidance, and intent.
      (2) Phase 2. Target development and prioritization.
      (3) Phase 3. Capabilities analysis.
      (4) Phase 4. Commander’s decision and force assignment.
      (5) Phase 5. Mission planning and force execution.
      (6) Phase 6. Combat assessment.
   b. Targeting Guidance. Phases 1 through 4 of the JTC collectively produce various
   products and perform a variety of tasks, whether deliberate or dynamic. In
   coordination with component headquarters (HQ) and combat support agencies, the
   joint force commander (JFC) and staff develop DT guidance. This guidance should
   include (at a minimum) priorities, guidance, and requirements for the component
   HQ, guidance for acquisition of specific targets, and specific actions against those
   targets. The JFC should articulate a tolerance of risk in sufficient detail for the
   tactical or supported commanders to understand commander’s intent when DT
   requires accelerated coordination, reprioritization, or reallocation of collection or
   attack assets.
   c. Joint DT. The majority of the joint DT process is normally executed during phase
   5 of the JTC. The DT process is a condensed execution phase that is not
   determined by the type of target being engaged. During the DT process, targets are
   prosecuted outside of the 24-hour integrated target order (ITO)/air tasking order
   (ATO) cycle using the condensed process of find, fix, track, target, engage, and
   assess (F2T2EA). See chapter IV for a more in-depth discussion of the below steps.
      (1) Find. Detect and classify targets for prosecution.
      (2) Fix. Determine the location of the potential target.
      (3) Track. Observe and monitor the target’s activity and movement.
      (4) Target. Decide whether to engage the target. Select and coordinate the
      means to create the desired effects.
      (5) Engage. Take action against the target.

    d. Alternative Targeting Processes. Service components, US special operations,

2. Targeting Categories
    a. Per Joint Publication (JP) 3-60, Joint Targeting, the two targeting categories
    within the JTC are deliberate and dynamic. Deliberate targeting produces scheduled
    targets or on-call targets which are typically on a time horizon that fits within the
    commander’s decision-making cycle. Dynamic targeting prosecutes unscheduled
    and/or unanticipated targets that are focused on the near and current battle within a
    compressed timeline. The DT process is integrated within the JTC. For unscheduled
    targets, DT is aided or enhanced by deliberate targeting. While no planning or
    deliberate targeting is accomplished for unanticipated targets, the post engagement

 b. Deliberate Targeting (Planned). The deliberate targeting process provides a
 systematic analytical approach that focuses targeting efforts on supporting
 operational requirements and the commander’s objectives. It focuses the
 appropriate capabilities against adversary targets at the right time and place to
 impose specific desired effects that achieve joint force objectives. Deliberate
 targeting is a planning process for targets that are detected, identified, and
 developed in sufficient time to schedule actions against them in a specific tasking
 cycle. Planned targets are split into two categories, scheduled and on-call.
    (1) Scheduled Targets. Scheduled targets are prosecuted at a specific time and
    with specific capabilities. These targets should be captured on a specific target
    list and may appear on an ITO/ATO to synchronize actions. The diversion of
    planned resources could impact the creation of the desired effects during the
    targeting period and should be weighed against the commander’s targeting
    guidance.
    (2) On-call Targets. On-call targets have dedicated capabilities planned against
    them but not for a specific delivery time. The commander expects to locate these
    targets in sufficient time to prosecute them in a timely manner. These targets are
    unique in that actions are planned against them using deliberate targeting, but
    execution is normally conducted using DT inside of the current operations day
    (usually the current 24-hour execution period). Ground maneuver forces, ships,
    and submarines (e.g., mobile targets) are examples of deliberately planned on-
    call targets that are dynamically prosecuted. On-call targets require responsive
    fires and/or persistent surveillance to maintain reliable location updates. Once
    found, they need to be engaged quickly, since they may not be found again and,
    in 24 hours, they could be 500 nautical miles away. Validation during execution
    includes analysis of the situation to determine if planned targets still contribute to
    commander’s intent, if targets are accurately located, and how planned actions
    will impact friendly operations.
 c. DT (Target of Opportunity). Target validation is critical during DT. DT prosecutes
 targets identified too late, or not selected for action in time to be included in
 deliberate targeting. Targets of opportunity meet the criteria to achieve objectives
 but are not selected for action during the current JTC. Targets of opportunity split
 into two categories, unscheduled and unanticipated.

    engagement (ROE) authorities) that may reside on either the joint target list (JTL)
    or the restricted target list (RTL) that were neither nominated nor approved for
    inclusion on the joint integrated prioritized target list (JIPTL), or were not available
    for engagement within the current target cycle. However, changes to the target
    status (priority, access, permissions) could result in the need or opportunity to
    engage the target during current operations.
    (2) Unanticipated targets are targets either unknown or unexpected in the OE.
    These targets may not have been processed through deliberate targeting nor
    included on a JTL or RTL. An evaluation of the candidate target is required
    before it can be engaged. In some cases, the candidate target will be engaged in

3. Target Value Analysis and Prioritization
   a. Sensitive Targets. Sensitive targets are those targets for which planned actions
   warrant President or Secretary of Defense (SecDef) approval. Sensitive target
   criteria are normally delineated in plans, orders, or ROE by JFCs. Sensitive targets,
   which are typically on the RTL, may include those targets that, if struck, have a high
   probability of collateral damage, civilian harm, adverse political or diplomatic
   ramifications, environmental harm or hazard, or adverse public sentiment.
 b. High-Value Targets (HVTs). HVTs are targets the enemy requires for the
 achievement of their objectives. The loss of HVTs would be expected to seriously
 degrade important enemy capabilities or systems function throughout or beyond the
 enemy’s operational area (OA). HVT lists should describe the relative value of each
 target, which varies under specific conditions and over the course of operations.
 Targets from this list should be nominated for target development or action. HVTs
 are generated during phase 2 (target development and prioritization) of the JTC. For
 additional information, refer to JP 3-60.

 c. High-Payoff Targets (HPTs). The loss of a HPT to the enemy significantly
 contributes to the success of the joint force. HPTs are normally derived from a list of
 HVTs. HPTs are normally nominated by components of the joint force in a prioritized
 target nomination list (TNL) during phase 2 of the JTC. Due to their importance in
 support of the JFC’s objectives, the JFC and the components prioritize these targets.
 Time-sensitive targets (TSTs) and component-critical targets (CCTs) are usually
 special types of HPTs. At any level, from the combatant command (CCMD) and
 below, the commander may approve a HPT list. This list identifies HPTs, by time and
 space, that are vital to the on-scene commander within a given OA to create effects
 to support achievement of tactical objectives.
 d. TST. A TST is a JFC-validated target, or set of targets, of such high importance
 to the JFC’s objectives that the JFC dedicates or diverts intelligence assets and fires
 to engage the TST. TSTs require an immediate response because it is a highly
 lucrative and fleeting target of opportunity, or it poses (or will soon pose) a direct
 danger to friendly forces or the broader OE. TSTs are designated by the JFC and
 identified as such in the JFC’s CONOPS, operation order (also referred to as
 OPORD), and the JIPTL. The JFC creates a hierarchy of importance for TSTs.
 Depending on the OE, they represent a very small portion of the total number of
 targets due to the required investment of assets and potential disruption to current
 execution. TSTs typically require considerable deliberate planning and preparation
 and direct coordination with component and supporting commanders within the JTC.
 Unscheduled TSTs may emerge during the execution period and be serviced using
 DT.
 e. CCT. Component commanders may nominate HPTs to the JFC for consideration
 as TSTs. These targets, if not designated as TSTs by the JFC, may still require
 dynamic engagement and cross-component coordination, assistance, and
 deconfliction in time-compressed environments and are therefore designated as
 CCTs for the component. The component commanders should:
    (1) Identify CCTs within the JTC.
    (2) Provide clear guidance to ensure the appropriate priority of asset allocation
    (collection, exploitation, and engagement assets) for these targets.
    (3) Provide engagement criteria to facilitate rapid cross-component coordination
    to minimize confusion and expedite execution.

             COMMANDER’S OBJECTIVES AND GUIDANCE
1. Background
  a. Detailed commander’s objectives and guidance are the most important elements
  of effective DT. The JFC and their staff, in coordination with components and other
  agencies, develop DT guidance. The JFC’s guidance should articulate risk tolerance
  to help subordinate commanders execute DT. As a minimum, DT guidance from the
  JFC should include the following:
     (1) Priorities and requirement identification, by component.
     (2) Target prioritization.
     (3) Guidance for target acquisition including positive identification criteria.
     (4) Prescribed actions against certain target types.
     (5) Estimated effects on the civilian environment.
  b. The JFC provides guidance on objectives, desired effects, priorities, and
  acceptable levels of risk. In addition, the JFC provides force apportionments,
  guidance on munitions usage and restrictions, target list management, and no-strike
  considerations.
  c. Clear delineation of supported and supporting relationships among the JFC
  components is essential for effective DT operations. See JP 1, Volume 1, Joint
  Warfighting, JP 1 Volume 2, The Joint Force, and JP 5-0, Joint Planning, for more
  information on supported and supporting relationships.
2. DT Guidance and Authority
  a. JFC-level Centralized DT. The JFC may retain authority for DT or may delegate
  this command and control (C2) responsibility to a component. An example in which a
  JFC may retain authority to engage targets is when national policy or caveats
  require high levels of approval before execution. Sensitive political considerations,
  force protection, civilian harm, or high risk of negative publicity also need to be
  considered.
  b. Decentralized Planning and Execution Delegated to the Component Commander.
  The JFC may delegate the responsibilities and authorities for target planning,
  coordination, and execution to component commanders. If delegated, the
  component commander is responsible for establishing and providing resources for a
  DT cell or organization. These may include:
     (1) Fire support element (FSE)/fires cell (FC).
     (2) Supporting arms coordination center (SACC).
     (3) Strike warfare commander (STWC).
     (4) Sea combat commander (SCC).
     (5) Fires and effects coordination center (FECC).

3. JFC DT Guidance
  a. The output of phase 1 of the JTC is the JFC’s targeting guidance. DT guidance is
  nested within the JFC’s overall targeting guidance. The joint targeting methodology
  is an iterative process requiring JFC and component interaction during all phases of
  planning and execution. The process must include a feedback mechanism for the
  components to make change recommendations during planning. The interactive
  planning between the JFC and component commanders’ staffs ensures guidance is
  understood and executable while the engagement authority is designated to the
  appropriate level.
  b. The JFC, through the operations directorate (J-3) or joint fires element (JFE),
  reviews DT guidance periodically to ensure it remains appropriate and relevant. DT
  guidance should be a specific agenda item during joint targeting coordination boards
  (JTCBs). If a JTCB is not convened, the JFE reviews DT guidance on a deliberate
  schedule, accounting for the changing nature of the OE and the joint force
  objectives. Based on the commander’s decision-making cycle, the JFC may
  disseminate updated DT guidance in the form of component orders, ITO/ATO,
  fragmentary orders (also referred to as FRAGORDs), etc. The following items
  require JFC targeting guidance:
     (1) Collection Priorities. The JFC assigns collection priorities to meet DT
     collection requirements. Considerations in assigning assets for these collection
     priorities include national and component collection capabilities (with national
     caveats, if required) available for the operation. This guidance provides
     instruction to the joint collection management board, JFE, JFC staff, components,
     and the US intelligence community. The JFC’s collection management authority
     can reprioritize sensor taskings for dynamic targets. To enable the components to
     conduct DT effectively, the JFC can delegate collection operations management
     to the component. The components could then generate a reconnaissance,
     surveillance, and target acquisition (RSTA) plan or similar document to prioritize
     the intelligence, surveillance, and reconnaissance (ISR) assets within their
     control. The RSTA plan serves as component-level guidance for dynamic tasking
     capabilities and identifies appropriate authorities within each component.
     (2) Target Development Standards. Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff
     Instruction (CJCSI) 3370.01C, Target Development Standards, outlines target
     development standards used in deliberate and dynamic targeting. Individuals
     qualified in target development are required to develop sufficient intelligence to
     support target validation, even when planning necessitates a truncated target
     system analysis process as found in DT. For DT, a target is considered “fully
     developed” when sufficient information exists to support the operational and legal
     requirements necessary to proceed with military operations against it.

1. Introduction
DT requires coordination and synchronization across all components and supporting
agencies. DT is inherent in and essential to operations in a joint all-domain environment
as described in JP 1. This chapter explains the C2 organizations, relationships, and
best practices from recent combat operations, exercises, and joint doctrine updates. It
also provides examples of component C2 organizations.
2. Responsibilities of the Supported/Supporting Commanders
   a. JFC Responsibilities. The JFC develops plans and orders IAW the JPP as
   outlined in JP 5-0. As such, the JFC issues guidelines and priorities and may
   delegate planning responsibilities and authorities. The JFC establishes command
   relationships which include supported and supporting relationships between
   components to facilitate unity of command and unified action.
   b. Component Commander Responsibilities. Component commanders establish
   planning and C2 organizations and define the internal and collaborative procedures
   for conducting DT. DT cells share situational awareness (SA) of the OE via wide-
   area networks, shared common operational picture (COP), and exchange of
   component liaisons.
      (1) Supported commanders provide clearly defined fire support priorities to the
      supporting commanders. Supporting commanders provide the support needed
      while clearly disclosing existing capabilities and limitations which add risk to a
      mission.
      (2) JP 3-09 discusses supported and supporting relationships for joint fire
      support within the joint operations area (JOA) and across AO boundaries. The
      following are examples of operations that require increased coordination.
          (a) A component detects a target within the JOA or in its AO and requests
          support from another component to create the desired effect. For example,
          the joint force land component commander (JFLCC) requests support from
          the joint force air component commander (JFACC). The supported
          component commander retains responsibility for deconfliction of fires and
          maneuver forces within their AO. Airspace must be coordinated with other
          affected forces.

                                       Chapter IV
                      DYNAMIC TARGETING EXECUTION
1. Introduction
As discussed in detail in Chapter I, the JTC is the process which sets the targeting
guidance for effective DT. While the JTC does prosecute deliberate planned targets and
dynamic targets of opportunity, the detailed procedures for dynamic targets are
separate and distinctly different from the other phases of the JTC. Examples within this
chapter illustrate target development and execution as it applies to current operations.
2. DT Within the JTC
   a. Below is a review of the six phases of the JTC and a brief explanation of how DT
   may be incorporated throughout the steps (as applicable). For a more in-depth
   discussion of the JTC, refer to chapter I.
      (1) Phase 1: Commander’s Objectives, Targeting Guidance, and Intent.
      Commander’s guidance defines the campaign objectives to attain the desired end
      state. Component commanders interpret the JFC’s guidance and provide
      recommendations to achieve JFC objectives.
      (2) Phase 2: Target Development and Prioritization.
          (a) Target development is the JFC and component-level analysis,
          assessment, and documentation processes that identifies and characterizes
          potential targets that, when successfully engaged, support the achievement of
          the commander’s objectives. Target development considers targeting threat
          networks but also consider a wide spectrum of targets that support the JFC
          scheme of maneuver. See JP 3-25, Joint Countering Threat Networks, for
          more information.
          (b) Components submit a candidate target list (also referred to as CTL) for
          inclusion on the JTL and/or RTL and to update target data in the modernized
          integrated database (MIDB). Components nominate JTL/RTL targets for
          execution by submitting a TNL for consideration via the JFC’s target approval
          process and for and inclusion on the JIPTL. Targets approved for inclusion on
          the JIPTL are scheduled for prosecution at a specific time. However,
          deliberately planned on-call targets that normally require updates and targets
          of opportunity (unanticipated or unscheduled) identified during current
          operations will normally be executed using DT.
          (c) Targeting planners identify potential targets for consideration as TSTs,
          HPTs, and CCTs.
      (3) Phase 3: Capabilities Analysis. Components preplan and weaponeer targets
      for deliberate and dynamic execution. Planning considerations include:
          (a) Available capabilities to create lethal and nonlethal effects.

  a. Coordination and Control Measures. With the rapid pace of operations, the joint
  force HQ, components, and other US departments and agencies should be capable
  of disseminating and implementing near real-time target coordination and control
  measures. Staffs and HQ identify, disseminate, and rehearse coordination and
  control measures between components. Components use joint approved control
  measures for planned and operational requirements. For detailed descriptions of
  control measures, see JP 3-09, Joint Fire Support.
  b. The supported commander plans, integrates, and synchronizes joint all domain
  operations across the joint functions in coordination with supporting commanders.
  Coordination is a shared responsibility, between supported and supporting
  components conducted through iterative planning.
     (1) First, the supported unit must accurately locate the target.
     (2) Second, the supported unit must consider all coordination and control
     measures, intended and unintended effects.

  d. ISR Coordination. ISR sensors operate at multiple security levels and come from

  operations, intelligence, and multinational forces to pass data between security
  levels in a secure and timely manner. ISR spans the entire DT process and

4. F2T2EA
  a. F2T2EA is a condensed execution phase function that is not determined by the
  type of target being engaged (see figure 5). Though primarily a DT function, its
  applicability could extend to all targets, whether developed during deliberate
  planning (scheduled, on-call) or during current operations (unscheduled,
  unanticipated). Due to the fleeting nature, or time sensitivity, these targets of
  opportunity decisions on when and how to engage are made quickly and decisively.
  The steps of F2T2EA may be accomplished iteratively, in parallel, or during
  deliberate planning (i.e. on-call targets). The find, fix, track, and assess steps tend to
  be ISR-intensive, while the target and engage steps are typically operations-
  intensive. In countering threat networks, the find, fix, finish, exploit, analyze, and
  disseminate (F3EAD) methodology process is used and can support DT of network
  nodes, when timing is crucial but also follow-on targeting through timely exploitation
  and analysis. During a peer fight, when large numbers of targets may appear in the
  OE without warning, the DT process is still applicable.

               DYNAMIC TARGETING (DT) ATTACK RESOURCES
1. Overview
   a. This appendix serves as a quick reference of attack assets that may be available
   to target, engage, and assess targets as part of DT. Assets listed in this appendix
   cover present United States (US) military systems. There may be additional assets,
   with unique capabilities, available from multinational partners and nonmilitary
   agencies, however, they are not discussed in this appendix.
Note: Due to the ever-changing capabilities and availability of Department of Defense
and multinational resources, it is impractical to produce and maintain a current listing of
all possible assets for DT. Therefore, this appendix is meant to be a sample, quick
reference list that DT planners should produce prior to the start of hostilities. The major
sources of information to produce a current and relevant list of DT resources will be the
subject matter experts from the components, US departments and agencies, and liaison
officers (LNOs) assigned to the DT cell.
   b. The distinction of traditional shooters and sensors is not absolute. In many cases,
   attack assets also have intelligence, surveillance, and reconnaissance (ISR)
   capabilities (e.g., targeting systems on fighter aircraft). Platforms originally designed
   for an ISR role may carry weapons. The key is to understand the capabilities of each
   asset and be ready to maximize its capabilities to meet DT requirements.
   c. A wide range of capabilities to deliver lethal and nonlethal effects in support of the
   joint force commander’s (JFC’s) objectives are available. The selection of assets for
   engaging targets may be significantly influenced by factors such as the JFC’s
   desired effect; availability, capability, or engagement characteristics of weapon
   systems; space and terrestrial weather conditions; collateral damage; and rules of
   engagement.
2. Artillery
   a. Cannon Artillery. Cannon artillery is an available surface-to-surface fire support
   system within the area of operations (AO). However, range limitations prevent these
   systems from striking operationally or strategically relevant targets. Cannon artillery
   provides near immediate response times and 24-hour, all-weather availability, and
   360-degree coverage.
      (1) Cannons offer area and precision fires. The Excalibur is a precision-guided,
      extended range 155 millimeter (mm), high-explosive cannon artillery projectile
      with an integrated Global Positioning System (GPS)-inertial navigation system
      guidance and unitary warhead. It provides artillery capabilities in urban areas and
      restrictive terrain, while minimizing collateral damage.
      (2) Excalibur is compatible with the M109A6 and M109A7 Paladin, self-propelled
      howitzer and the M777A2, lightweight (155mm), towed Howitzer. A precision
      guidance kit is a trajectory-correcting fuse kit compatible with standard 155mm
      munitions. It has the following advantages.

1. Overview

2. JFSOCC Command and Control (C2) Procedures and Structure Example
   a. The JFSOCC should establish procedures to nominate and deconflict targets
   throughout the joint operations area. These procedures must conform to joint force
   commander (JFC) guidance and be published in an order promulgated throughout
   the joint force.
   b. There are two ways to nominate targets. Either the JFSOCC will nominate a
   target within its operating area, or another force will nominate a target for
   surveillance and potential strike by special operations forces (SOF) within the
   JFSOCC operating area. In either case, the JFSOCC joint fires element (JFE) will
   deconflict all SOF and the JFSOCC will determine if any SOF are capable of
   prosecuting the target to create the desired effect.
   c. The JFSOCC communications system directorate of a joint staff (J-6) must
   determine how forces will communicate identified target information to the JFE. The
   most rapid method with the least number of handoffs is preferred. The most direct
   way is through dedicated communication networks (e.g., satellite communications or
   high frequency). The JFSOCC’s current operations JFE will monitor the SOF-
   dedicated communication network to maintain instant communications capability.
   The JFSOCC’s subcomponents also have access to this network and monitor
   communications to maximize allow situational awareness (SA) (see figures 7 and 8
   for all JFSOCC subcomponents). The JFSOCC’s JFE and subcomponents will
   conduct two parallel tasks: deconfliction and a feasibility assessment.
   d. To deconflict, the JFSOCC JFE will check the common operational picture for
   other SOF. It is critical for the JFE to contact other JFSOCC subcomponents to
   check their forces’ locations. The JFSOCC and subcomponents monitoring
   dedicated communication networks should start this process upon hearing the DT
   notification. The criteria to determine if a SOF team is too close to allow a target
   strike is based on many factors including: type of target, estimation of the weapon or
   system which may be used for the target (i.e., the size of required ordnance), cover
   for the team, and mobility of the team.

                       Appendix D
   MULTINATIONAL DYNAMIC TARGETING (DT) CONSIDERATIONS
1. Introduction
This appendix highlights multinational issues with DT and provides generic guidance for
commanders and their staffs. Effective planning and coordination should result with the
best use of multinational capabilities.
2. Command and Control (C2)
   a. When multinational partners stand up a national contingent headquarters (HQ), it
   is responsible to their national command authorities, not the coalition HQ. The
   coalition HQ is separate from the national contingent HQ and is responsible to the
   coalition commander. Multinational partners may be dual-hatted to the coalition HQ
   and the national contingent HQ. The purpose of the national HQ is to liaise with their
   national command authorities when approval for DT issues is needed. Ideally, the
   multinational HQ will be collocated with the coalition HQ.
   b. There are three ways in which coalition personnel may be expected to operate as
   part of DT.
      (1) A Member of an Integrated HQ. This HQ staff is integrated from the troop-
      contributing nations of the coalition. Personnel who are full-time members of a
      permanent, multinational staff may not have access to all DT management tools
      and may not operate alongside their HQ counterparts. Augmentees to the staff
      will have access to the DT tools but must be educated on system usage.
      (2) Liaison Personnel. These personnel serve as interfaces between national
      and coalition HQ for DT coordination.
      (3) A Member of a National HQ. This HQ member is responsible for liaising
      between the national command authority and the coalition HQ on DT issues.
   c. If coalition personnel are integrated within a DT cell, they will perform similar roles
   as lead-nation personnel in the same posts. Coalition personnel can offer advice and
   expertise. The coalition element commanders at the component HQ represent
   national interests. There may be two or more responsibilities in circumstances where
   the multinational commanders have deputized roles for their United States (US)
   counterparts.
   d. It may not be necessary to have multinational representation from every country
   in coalition DT cells. Some nations may not be represented in the component HQ or
   they may be restricted to preclude their participation in DT activities. Multinational
   involvement within a DT cell depends on which multinational forces are apportioned
   to the task. Also, these nations may require positioning liaison officers (LNOs). The
   degree of involvement will depend on command relationships.

INFORMATION CAPABILITIES PLANNING FOR DYNAMIC TARGETING

1. Introduction
The use of information capabilities, either during deliberate or dynamic targeting, is no
small task to place upon the staff. A lot of the information capabilities in the United
States (US) inventory are exquisite and sensitive. They are protected through multiple
layers of operations security to ensure protection of the capability and tactics,
techniques, and procedures (TTP) that are used to employ the specific capability. All of
this means that the utilization of information capabilities requires deliberate planning to
ensure all variables are considered in the use of information capabilities. Therefore, the
following are some learned planning considerations to incorporate information
capabilities into DT.
2. Elements of Information Capabilities
All planning and execution of information capabilities begins with four requirements:
authorities, placement, access, and capability.
   a. Authorities. Every action conducted by the Department of Defense (DoD) is
   authorized by an authority (i.e., Title 50 authorizes intelligence gathering on specific
   threats and/or adversaries). For this reason, the integration of information
   capabilities requires the understanding of authorities that the commander has been
   delegated to conduct operations in support of achieving specific objectives. Each
   type of information capability has a different type of authority to prepare, plan,
   execute, and assess the conduct of those fires, normally at the JFC level or above.
   Therefore, a fires planner must understand that these authorities are not requested,
   reviewed, and approved in an expedited manner in most cases. To ensure that one
   has the authorities to execute fires using information capabilities, the usage of
   information capabilities must be identified, planned for, and requested early in the
   deliberate planning process and/or understand the process to execute these fires
   utilizing higher and/or adjacent authorities.
   b. Placement. Defined as, “the action of putting someone or something in a
   particular place”, information capabilities require authorities for forces and equipment
   to be pushed forward to the edge of battle. The edge of battle can be in any domain
   or environment (i.e., the boundary going into red space from gray space in the
   cyberspace domain). The ability to conduct placement requires authorities to do so
   and the physical or logical location of placement needs to be identified early or as
   early as feasibly possible.
   c. Access. Placement provides proximity to the target, but the information capability
   must also have access to the battlespace (term is inclusive of all domains and
   environments) in which they are executing. For example, a military information
   support operations (MISO) team is in district Y of country X but not within line of
   sight of a radio tower to broadcast approved MISO messages. This means that the
   capability is unable to create effects against the target. The access to target space

SPACE CONSIDERATIONS SUPPORTING DYNAMIC TARGETING (DT)
1. Overview
Space assets have a variety of delivery options that possess the ability to impact
ground, link, and space segment. Integrating space assets for DT may consist of long-
term preparation and real-time tactical mission planning during the execution phase of
the integrated tasking order/air tasking order. The processes to create space effects
involved with target development and deployment/repositioning, as well as the potential
for inability to access a target, will require coordination emphasis to focus on long-term
preparation and planning for anticipated targets and effects to meet commander
objectives. For additional information, reference Joint Publication 3-14, Joint Space
Operations.
2. Coordination for Space Fires
The joint force space component commander (JFSCC) will coordinate for space effects
in conjunction with the joint integrated space team (also referred to as JIST) in support
of operations requirements. The regional space component commander is the senior
advisor for coordinating effects to support theater requirements. The space component
commander and dedicated staff recommend whether space forces are assigned to or
supporting the combatant command (CCMD) and if those forces are equipped with the
required capabilities. A space control coordination element (also referred to as SCCE)
from the Combined Space Operations Center (CSPOC) may also be made available to
synchronize timing and tempo of theater operations with CSPOC operations in the
affected area of responsibility.
3. Authority to Employ Space Capabilities
   a. The authorities for space effects may be granted through a review and approval
   process (RAP). The RAP is the process to request space effects and includes
   coordination among stakeholders such as the supported CCMD, the intelligence
   community, other government agencies, and the Department of Defense. The RAP
   is approved by the Secretary of Defense or the President of the United States, and
   the output is a deployment order or execute order.
   b. Limitations. To generate a dynamic effect in support of DT, a request for the
   effect must have previously undergone the RAP protocol as mentioned above. If a
   RAP request is not completed, the asset required to support DT may not be
   available as needed due to lack of directive execution authority.
4. Planning and Coordination of Space Effects for DT
There are different processes in each CCMD for planning and requesting space effects
for DT. Typically, the regional space force component will aid in planning and
coordinating space effects for DT.

       called JTC. (Source: JP 3-60).
